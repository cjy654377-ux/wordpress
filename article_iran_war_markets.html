The Day Global Markets Held Their Breath

February 28, 2026 will be studied in business schools for decades. In just 12 hours, Operation Epic Fury unleashed nearly 900 strikes against Iranian targets, killing Supreme Leader Khamenei and effectively shutting down the Strait of Hormuz — the narrow waterway through which 20% of the world's oil and liquefied natural gas passes every single day.

The financial fallout was immediate and brutal. Brent crude rocketed from $70 to $126 per barrel. The S&P 500 tumbled to 5,740. Gold blasted past $5,400 an ounce. And more than 150 ships sat anchored outside the Strait, unable to pass through one of the most critical chokepoints in global commerce.

Now, nearly two weeks later, President Trump declared the operation "very complete, pretty much" on March 10 — and markets responded with a collective exhale. European indices climbed 1.5%, Asia-Pacific surged 3.2%, and oil pulled back sharply from its peak.

But here is the uncomfortable truth: the damage is already baked in. Consumers are paying more for gas, groceries, and goods. The Fed is boxed in. And the structural risks — tariffs, supply chain disruptions, geopolitical uncertainty — are not going away just because the shooting may be stopping.

This article breaks down exactly what happened to every major asset class, where things stand today, and what smart investors should be doing right now. If you missed our earlier coverage, start with Oil Prices Surge 8% as Iran Conflict Threatens Strait of Hormuz for the initial shock, and 12 Ways the Iran War Is Reshaping the World Right Now for the broader geopolitical picture.

The speed of the pullback is remarkable. Brent has given back roughly two-thirds of its war premium in under two weeks. But before you celebrate at the gas pump, understand what is actually happening beneath the surface.

The IEA called an emergency meeting to coordinate a strategic petroleum reserve release among member nations. This is the same playbook used during the 2011 Libya crisis and briefly after Russia's invasion of Ukraine. It works — temporarily. SPR releases create artificial supply that pushes prices down, but they drain reserves that exist precisely for worst-case scenarios.

Meanwhile, the Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed. Those 150+ ships are not moving. Even if Iran agrees to a ceasefire tomorrow, reopening the Strait involves minesweeping, insurance renegotiation, and a slow ramp-up of tanker traffic. We are talking weeks, not days.

The real risk? Iran's Foreign Minister Araghchi rejected ceasefire talks outright: "They have to explain why they started this aggression." As long as that posture holds, the Strait stays dangerous, and the oil market stays on edge.

For energy investors, the trade has shifted. The initial panic spike was a momentum play. Now it is about fundamentals: how quickly can alternative supply routes and SPR releases offset the Hormuz bottleneck, and how long does Iran remain defiant?

Stock Markets: The S&P 500 at a Crossroads

The S&P 500's drop to 5,740 was sharp but, in historical terms, relatively contained — roughly a 5-6% correction from recent highs. The bigger concern is what the valuation backdrop looked like before any of this happened.

The Shiller CAPE ratio — the gold standard for measuring whether stocks are expensive relative to long-term earnings — stood at 39.0 heading into the conflict. For context, the historical average is about 17. The only times the CAPE was higher were the dot-com bubble (44.2 in December 1999) and a brief period in late 2021.

JPMorgan is now warning of a potential 10% correction from here. Let that sink in. We are not talking about a pullback from the peak — we are talking about another 10% from already-reduced levels. That would put the S&P 500 in the low 5,100s, wiping out most of the gains from 2025.

Morgan Stanley's latest note is telling: they are recommending clients increase exposure to defense, security, and aerospace stocks. This is not a short-term trade. Even if the Iran conflict ends cleanly, global defense budgets were already trending upward. This crisis just put rocket fuel on that trajectory.

The March 10 relief rally — Europe up 1.5%, Asia-Pacific up 3.2% — shows how desperate markets are for good news. But a rally built on a single presidential quote is fragile. The underlying problems (elevated valuations, tariff drag, oil uncertainty) have not been solved by Trump saying the operation is "pretty much" complete.

Gold, Bitcoin, and the Safe Haven Myth

Every crisis tests the "safe haven" thesis, and the 2026 Iran war has delivered a clear verdict: gold is still king, and Bitcoin is not digital gold — at least not yet.

Gold did exactly what gold is supposed to do. It surged to $5,400 per ounce — within striking distance of the all-time high of $5,595 — as investors fled to the one asset that has held its value through wars, pandemics, and financial crises for 5,000 years. JPMorgan forecasts gold at $5,055 by Q4 2026, which implies some pullback but still historically elevated levels.

Bitcoin told a very different story. It dropped to $63,000 during the initial panic — a move that looks even worse when you consider it hit an all-time high of $126,000 just five months ago in October 2025. That is a 47% drawdown from peak. Bitcoin ETFs hemorrhaged $3.8 billion in outflows in February alone.

The Bitcoin community argued for years that BTC would shine in exactly this kind of scenario — geopolitical instability, currency debasement fears, institutional distrust. Instead, it traded like a levered Nasdaq position. When fear spiked, institutions sold Bitcoin right alongside growth stocks. The "digital gold" narrative took a serious hit.

That said, Bitcoin's recovery to the $69,500-71,000 range shows it has not collapsed entirely. It is acting more like a speculative risk asset with crypto-specific demand dynamics than either a safe haven or a pure tech proxy. Investors need to categorize it accordingly in their portfolios.

The Tariff Compounding Effect: Why This Crisis Hits Harder Than Past Wars

Here is what makes the 2026 Iran war uniquely dangerous for the global economy: it landed on top of the most aggressive tariff regime since the 1930s.

The effective US tariff rate has surged from less than 3% to approximately 18%. To put that in perspective, you have to go back to the Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act of 1930 — the one that worsened the Great Depression — to find comparable protectionist levels.

This is the compounding effect that makes the current situation so treacherous. Tariffs were already raising consumer prices before a single bomb dropped. The oil shock from the Hormuz shutdown piled gasoline and energy costs on top. And because tariffs make imported goods more expensive while oil makes domestic production and transportation costlier, the two forces reinforce each other.

The result is a potential stagflation trap. Growth slows because consumers have less purchasing power. But inflation stays elevated because cost pressures come from multiple directions simultaneously. This is the Fed's nightmare scenario, and it is exactly where we are heading if the situation does not improve quickly.

The Fed's Impossible Choice: March FOMC Preview

The Federal Reserve's March 17-18 FOMC meeting is shaping up to be one of the most consequential in recent memory. And the most likely outcome? Absolutely nothing.

The Fed is expected to hold the federal funds rate at 3.5%-3.75%. Markets now price in only one rate cut for all of 2026, a dramatic reversal from the three to four cuts that were expected at the start of the year.

Here is the dilemma in simple terms:

The case for cutting: The economy faces a genuine shock. Oil prices spiked, consumer confidence is cratering, and the S&P 500 is flashing warning signs. Financial conditions are tightening on their own. A rate cut would signal support and potentially prevent a self-fulfilling recession.

The case for holding: Inflation is being pushed higher by both the oil shock and tariffs. Cutting rates into an inflationary supply shock is how you get 1970s-style stagflation. The Fed learned that lesson the hard way under Arthur Burns. Jerome Powell has studied that history. He is not going to repeat it.

The case for hiking: Nobody is seriously proposing this, but the inflation data may eventually force the conversation. If core PCE stays above 3% and oil remains elevated, the Fed could face a scenario where holding rates is not tight enough.

The bottom line: expect the Fed to hold and use carefully worded language about "monitoring developments" and "data dependency." Translation: they are as uncertain as everyone else, and they are choosing to wait rather than make a potentially catastrophic mistake in either direction.

For investors, this means the rate-sensitive playbook (long duration bonds, growth stocks, REITs) is on hold. Do not count on rate cuts to bail out overvalued assets. The Fed put — the implicit backstop that many investors have relied on for over a decade — has an expiration date, and we may be approaching it.

The Bigger Picture: A World Repricing Risk

Step back from the daily market moves and a larger pattern emerges. The 2026 Iran war is not an isolated event — it is the latest in a series of shocks that are forcing global markets to reprice geopolitical risk after decades of relative complacency.

The IMF's global growth forecast of 3.3% was set before the conflict began. Goldman Sachs has already cut to 2.8%. The recession probability of 30% may sound manageable, but consider that we are layering war risk on top of the highest tariff environment in 90 years, commercial real estate stress, and AI-driven market concentration that has made the S&P 500 heavily dependent on a handful of mega-cap tech stocks.

The era of cheap energy, free trade, and geopolitical stability that powered the post-Cold War bull market is giving way to something messier. Tariffs are replacing free trade agreements. Wars are disrupting energy flows. Central banks are constrained by inflation they cannot fully control.

This does not mean markets are doomed. Stocks have generated positive returns through every major war in the past century. But the easy-money, low-volatility environment that most investors under 45 have known for their entire careers is changing. Risk premiums are going up. Valuations need to come down. And the investors who thrive will be the ones who adapt to this new reality rather than waiting for the old one to come back.

The Iran war may end soon. The repricing it triggered will not.

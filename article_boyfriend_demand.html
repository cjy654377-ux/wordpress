Plot — When Virtual Romance Meets Reality

Mi-rae (played by Jisoo) is a webtoon producer who knows everything about crafting the perfect love story — except how to live one. Her real office life is messy, competitive, and dominated by her cold, secretive colleague Kyeong-nam (Seo In-guk). To cope, she logs into a cutting-edge virtual dating service that offers fully customized fantasy scenarios tailored to each user's ideal type.

The program does not just give you a generic AI boyfriend. It lets you choose your type, your scenario, your pace. Think of it as the ultimate subscription box — except instead of skincare samples, you get a handcrafted romantic experience.

The tension builds as the gap between Mi-rae's polished virtual world and her imperfect real one widens. Kyeong-nam, meanwhile, is hiding something. The drama leans into the central question that defines modern romance: Can a perfect fantasy actually prepare you for a deeply imperfect reality?

The drama is based on a webtoon concept and written by Namgung Do-young, whose work consistently explores how digital life reshapes human connection. Director Kim Jung-sik is known for his visually driven storytelling and control of comedic timing.

The Virtual Boyfriend Lineup — 8 Dream Dates

This is where Boyfriend on Demand becomes genuinely unlike anything Netflix has aired in the K-Drama space. Instead of one male lead, the show deploys eight — each representing a different fantasy archetype tailored to Mi-rae's shifting emotional states.

The eight virtual boyfriends are:

  • Seo Kang-joon — The gentle, warm type audiences fell for in Are You Human Too?
  • Lee Soo-hyuk — Tall, intense, and visually commanding; the brooding archetype
  • Ong Seong-wu — Former Wanna One member bringing idol-polished charm
  • Lee Jae-wook — Rising drama star known for supernatural and fantasy roles
  • Lee Hyun-wook — The dependable, mature presence type
  • Kim Young-dae — Fresh-faced and earnest; the boy-next-door scenario
  • Jay Park — Hip-hop artist and entrepreneur adding unexpected edge
  • Lee Sang-yi — Comedy-drama veteran who brings warmth and wit

Each persona represents a curated fantasy scenario — different settings, different emotional tones, different "types." The drama uses this lineup not just as eye candy but as a structural device: each virtual date reveals something about what Mi-rae is actually running from in her real life.

"The virtual boyfriend service doesn't just give you romance. It gives you a mirror." — Thematic core of Boyfriend on Demand

The inclusion of Jay Park is a particularly interesting casting choice. He is not a traditional actor, which means his persona likely leans into his real-world identity — making the boundary between "virtual character" and "real person" deliberately blurry. That meta-layer is smart writing.

Can Jisoo Silence the Critics?

Let's address the elephant in the room. Jisoo made her acting debut in the 2022 Disney+ series Snowdrop, and the reception to her performance was polarizing. Critics and viewers raised concerns about limited range, and the "idol-turned-actress" skepticism has followed her into this project.

Here is what we know ahead of the premiere:

  • Director Kim Jung-sik confirmed at the February 26, 2026 press conference that Jisoo dominates screen time — this is her show, front and center
  • Mi-rae is written as a woman who is emotionally intelligent in fiction but emotionally avoidant in life — a character with real internal complexity
  • Jisoo has had four years of additional experience since Snowdrop, and the role was reportedly developed with her strengths in mind
  • The romantic comedy format is often more forgiving of naturalistic, less theatrical performances — which may play to her advantage

The debate is legitimate, but it is also worth remembering that several Blackpink members have successfully expanded into acting. The question is not whether Jisoo can act — it is whether this role was built to showcase what she can do.

The real test: Jisoo's scenes opposite Seo In-guk will define the critical conversation. He is a veteran with strong comedic and dramatic range. If their chemistry lands, the skeptics lose their primary argument.

Fan communities are already split into two camps: those who are watching to support Jisoo regardless, and those watching specifically to evaluate her. Both groups will generate enormous viewership numbers — which may be the most honest thing anyone has said about how K-Drama fandom works in 2026.

The office backdrop is a smart creative choice. It grounds the fantasy in something universally relatable — the colleague you cannot read, the job that drains you, the version of yourself you perform at work versus who you are at home.

If the writing follows through on its premise, Boyfriend on Demand has the potential to be the K-Drama that defines the conversation about digital intimacy in 2026. Not because it has answers — but because it is asking the right questions.

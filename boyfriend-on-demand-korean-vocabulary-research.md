## CATEGORY 1: Romance & Dating Expressions (Boyfriend on Demand Core Vocabulary)

### 1. 남자친구 (namja-chingu) — Boyfriend
- **Literal meaning:** "Male friend"
- **Context:** The title itself (월간남친) uses the abbreviated form 남친 (namchin). Mi-rae subscribes to literally choose her ideal boyfriend every month.
- **Example:** 남자친구 생겼어? (Namja-chingu saenggyeosseo?) — "Did you get a boyfriend?"
- **Drama connection:** The entire premise revolves around selecting the perfect 남자친구 through a virtual service.

### 2. 남친 (namchin) — Boyfriend (shortened)
- **Context:** Casual, abbreviated form of 남자친구. Used constantly among friends.
- **Example:** 내 남친이 최고야! (Nae namchin-i choego-ya!) — "My boyfriend is the best!"

### 3. 여자친구 / 여친 (yeoja-chingu / yeochin) — Girlfriend
- **Context:** Same structure as namja-chingu but for females.
- **Example:** 여친 있어요? (Yeochin isseoyo?) — "Do you have a girlfriend?"

### 4. 썸타다 (sseom-tada) — "In the talking stage" / having romantic tension
- **Derived from:** English word "something" — there's *something* between them
- **Context:** The ambiguous phase before officially dating. Light flirting, increased texting, maybe holding hands, but no label yet. This PERFECTLY describes Mi-rae's situation with her coworker Kyeong-nam.
- **Example:** 우리 지금 썸타는 거야? (Uri jigeum sseom-taneun geoya?) — "Are we in the talking stage right now?"
- **Drama connection:** Mi-rae and Kyeong-nam's entire real-world relationship arc is classic 썸.

### 5. 소개팅 (sogaeting) — Blind date
- **Context:** A date arranged by a mutual friend. Extremely common in Korean dating culture and K-dramas.
- **Example:** 내일 소개팅 나가. (Naeil sogaeting naga.) — "I'm going on a blind date tomorrow."
- **Drama connection:** The virtual dating service is essentially an AI-powered 소개팅 system.

### 6. 사귀다 (sagwida) — To officially date / be in a relationship
- **Context:** The step AFTER 썸타다. You mutually acknowledge the relationship. This is a big deal in Korean dating culture.
- **Example:** 우리 사귀자! (Uri sagwija!) — "Let's date!" / "Let's make it official!"

### 7. 심쿵 (simkkung) — Heart fluttering / heart skipping a beat
- **From:** 심장 (simjang, heart) + 쿵 (kkung, thump sound)
- **Context:** That moment when someone does something so attractive your heart literally skips. Every virtual boyfriend scene would trigger this.
- **Example:** 그 장면 보고 심쿵했어! (Geu jangmyeon bogo simkkung-haesseo!) — "My heart skipped watching that scene!"

### 8. 밀당 (mildang) — Push and pull (in dating)
- **From:** 밀다 (milda, push) + 당기다 (danggida, pull)
- **Context:** The classic flirting game of showing interest then playing hard to get. Kyeong-nam's "surly, self-centered" persona is textbook 밀당.
- **Example:** 밀당 좀 그만해! (Mildang jom geumanhae!) — "Stop playing hard to get!"

### 9. 짝사랑 (jjaksarang) — Unrequited love / one-sided crush
- **Context:** Loving someone who doesn't (yet) love you back. A K-drama staple.
- **Example:** 짝사랑은 너무 아파. (Jjaksarang-eun neomu apa.) — "Unrequited love hurts so much."

### 10. 케미 (kemi) — Romantic chemistry
- **From:** English word "chemistry"
- **Context:** Used to describe the spark between two people (or actors). K-drama fans constantly discuss which couples have the best 케미.
- **Example:** 이 커플 케미 미쳤어! (I keopeul kemi michyeosseo!) — "This couple's chemistry is insane!"

### 11. 자기 (jagi) — Honey / Darling / Babe
- **Context:** Pet name used between couples, gender-neutral. You'll hear this in every romance K-drama.
- **Example:** 자기야, 사랑해. (Jagiya, saranghae.) — "Honey, I love you."

### 12. 금사빠 (geumsappa) — Someone who falls in love easily
- **Abbreviation of:** 금방 사랑에 빠지는 사람 (geumbang sarange ppajineun saram)
- **Context:** Mi-rae falling for a different virtual boyfriend every episode? Classic 금사빠.
- **Example:** 너 완전 금사빠야! (Neo wanjeon geumsappa-ya!) — "You totally fall in love too easily!"

## CATEGORY 2: Greetings & Daily Life

### 13. 안녕하세요 (annyeonghaseyo) — Hello (polite)
- **Context:** The most essential Korean greeting. Used with anyone you don't know well, older people, or in professional settings.
- **Example:** 안녕하세요, 처음 뵙겠습니다. (Annyeonghaseyo, cheoeum boepgesseumnida.) — "Hello, nice to meet you for the first time."

### 14. 안녕 (annyeong) — Hi / Bye (casual)
- **Context:** Informal version used with close friends and people your age or younger. Can mean both hello AND goodbye.
- **Example:** 안녕, 잘 지냈어? (Annyeong, jal jinaesseo?) — "Hey, have you been well?"

### 15. 오빠 (oppa) — Older brother (used by females)
- **Context:** A younger woman addressing an older male. Can be used for actual brothers, close male friends, or boyfriends. In dating contexts, calling someone "oppa" carries romantic/flirtatious undertones. Mi-rae might call her virtual boyfriends this.
- **Example:** 오빠, 어디 가? (Oppa, eodi ga?) — "Oppa, where are you going?"

### 16. 언니 (eonni) — Older sister (used by females)
- **Context:** A younger woman addressing an older female. Shows closeness and respect.
- **Example:** 언니, 같이 가자! (Eonni, gachi gaja!) — "Eonni, let's go together!"

### 17. 형 (hyeong) — Older brother (used by males)
- **Context:** A younger man addressing an older male. Common in workplace and friend dynamics.
- **Example:** 형, 도와줘! (Hyeong, dowajwo!) — "Hyeong, help me!"

### 18. 누나 (nuna) — Older sister (used by males)
- **Context:** A younger man addressing an older female. Can also carry romantic undertones.
- **Example:** 누나, 밥 먹었어? (Nuna, bap meogeosseo?) — "Nuna, have you eaten?"

## CATEGORY 3: Emotional Expressions

### 19. 대박 (daebak) — Amazing! / OMG! / Jackpot!
- **Context:** The ultimate K-drama reaction word. Expresses shock, amazement, or excitement. Works for positive AND negative surprises.
- **Example:** 대박! 이거 진짜야? (Daebak! Igeo jinjja-ya?) — "OMG! Is this for real?"
- **Drama connection:** Mi-rae's reaction when she first enters the virtual dating world.

### 20. 화이팅 (hwaiting) — "Fighting!" / You can do it!
- **From:** English word "fighting" (Korean pronunciation)
- **Context:** The most iconic Korean encouragement phrase. Used before exams, job interviews, competitions, or whenever someone needs a boost.
- **Example:** 시험 화이팅! (Siheom hwaiting!) — "Good luck on your exam!"

### 21. 괜찮아 (gwaenchana) — "It's okay" / "I'm fine"
- **Context:** Used constantly in K-dramas. Sometimes genuinely reassuring, sometimes said while clearly NOT okay (a classic K-drama moment).
- **Example:** 울지 마, 괜찮아. (Ulji ma, gwaenchana.) — "Don't cry, it's okay."

### 22. 진짜? / 정말? (jinjja? / jeongmal?) — "Really?" / "Seriously?"
- **Context:** 진짜 is more casual/surprised, 정말 is slightly more formal. Both express disbelief or confirmation-seeking.
- **Example:** 진짜? 그 사람이 고백했어? (Jinjja? Geu saram-i gobaek-haesseo?) — "Really? That person confessed?"

### 23. 보고 싶어 (bogo sipeo) — "I miss you"
- **Context:** Deeply emotional phrase used in romantic and close relationships. One of the most heart-wrenching K-drama lines.
- **Example:** 너무 보고 싶었어. (Neomu bogo sipeosseo.) — "I missed you so much."

### 24. 가지 마 (gaji ma) — "Don't go"
- **Context:** The classic dramatic K-drama moment — grabbing someone's wrist and saying "don't go." Expect this in at least one episode.
- **Example:** 제발 가지 마! (Jebal gaji ma!) — "Please don't go!"

### 25. 미안해 (mianhae) — "I'm sorry" (casual)
- **Context:** Informal apology between friends or people of similar age. Formal version: 죄송합니다 (joesonghamnida).
- **Example:** 미안해, 내가 잘못했어. (Mianhae, naega jalmothaesseo.) — "I'm sorry, it was my fault."

## CATEGORY 4: Food & Drink Ordering

### 26. 여기요! (yeogiyo!) — "Excuse me!" / "Over here!"
- **Context:** How you call a server in a Korean restaurant. You'll hear this in virtually every K-drama meal scene.
- **Example:** 여기요! 주문할게요. (Yeogiyo! Jumun-halgeyo.) — "Excuse me! I'd like to order."

### 27. 맛있다! (masitda!) — "It's delicious!"
- **Context:** The go-to food reaction. K-dramas are famous for making food look incredible.
- **Example:** 와, 이거 진짜 맛있다! (Wa, igeo jinjja masitda!) — "Wow, this is really delicious!"

### 28. 건배! (geonbae!) — "Cheers!"
- **Context:** Used when clinking glasses. Soju scenes are a K-drama staple — whether it's a celebration or a heartbreak drinking scene.
- **Example:** 우리의 사랑을 위해, 건배! (Uriui sarangeul wihae, geonbae!) — "To our love, cheers!"

### 29. 한잔 하자 (hanjan haja) — "Let's have a drink"
- **Context:** A casual invitation for drinks, usually soju or beer. Often happens after work (like Mi-rae and Kyeong-nam staying late at the office).
- **Example:** 오늘 힘들었지? 한잔 하자. (Oneul himdeureotji? Hanjan haja.) — "Today was tough, right? Let's have a drink."

### 30. 잘 먹겠습니다 (jal meokgesseumnida) — "I will eat well" (said before eating)
- **Context:** Korean table etiquette. Said before every meal to express gratitude for the food. After eating: 잘 먹었습니다 (jal meogeosseumnida) — "I ate well."
- **Example:** (sitting down at table) 잘 먹겠습니다! — "Thank you for the food!"

## CATEGORY 5: Slang & Trendy Expressions (2025-2026)

### 31. 꿀잼 (kkuljaem) — "So fun" / "Entertaining as honey"
- **From:** 꿀 (kkul, honey) + 재미 (jaemi, fun)
- **Context:** When something is SO entertaining it's sweet like honey. Opposite: 노잼 (nojaem) = no fun, boring.
- **Example:** 이 드라마 꿀잼이야! (I deurama kkuljaem-iya!) — "This drama is so entertaining!"

### 32. 찐 (jjin) — "Real" / "Genuine" / "The real deal"
- **Context:** Used to emphasize authenticity. 찐텐 (jjinten) = "real energy/excitement" (not faking it). Very popular in 2025-2026.
- **Example:** 이건 찐이야! (Igeon jjin-iya!) — "This is the real deal!"

### 33. 헐 (heol) — "OMG" / "No way" / "Whoa"
- **Context:** Korean equivalent of gasping in shock. Universal reaction word.
- **Example:** 헐, 말도 안 돼! (Heol, maldo an dwae!) — "OMG, no way!"

### 34. 현웃 (hyeonut) — LOL (literally laughing in real life)
- **From:** 현실 (hyeonsil, reality) + 웃음 (useum, laughter)
- **Context:** When something is so funny you actually laugh out loud, not just type "ㅋㅋㅋ."
- **Example:** 방금 그 장면 현웃 터졌어! (Banggeum geu jangmyeon hyeonut teojyeosseo!) — "I literally burst out laughing at that scene!"

### 35. 셀카 (selka) — Selfie
- **From:** 셀프 카메라 (selpeu kamera, self camera)
- **Context:** Korean word for selfie. Essential modern vocabulary.
- **Example:** 우리 셀카 찍자! (Uri selka jjikja!) — "Let's take a selfie!"

### 36. 쩐다 (jjeonda) — "So cool" / "Awesome" / "Impressive"
- **Context:** Expresses being deeply impressed by something. Slang, used among younger people.
- **Example:** 쩐다! 어떻게 그렇게 잘해? (Jjeonda! Eotteoke geureoke jalhae?) — "That's awesome! How are you so good at that?"

### 37. 불금 (bulgeum) — TGIF / "Burning Friday"
- **From:** 불타는 금요일 (bultaneun geumyoil, burning Friday)
- **Context:** The excitement of Friday night. After a long week at the office, Mi-rae and her coworkers would be celebrating 불금.
- **Example:** 불금이다! 놀러 가자! (Bulgeum-ida! Nolleo gaja!) — "It's Friday! Let's go have fun!"

### 38. 모쏠 (mosol) — Someone who's never dated / "Solo since birth"
- **From:** 모태 솔로 (motae sollo, solo from the womb)
- **Context:** Describes someone who has never been in a relationship. They might be the type to subscribe to a virtual boyfriend service...
- **Example:** 나 아직 모쏠이야. (Na ajik mosol-iya.) — "I've never dated anyone."

### 39. 훈남 (hunnam) — Warm, handsome, well-mannered man
- **Context:** Not just good-looking but has a warm, approachable vibe. The ideal virtual boyfriend type.
- **Example:** 저 사람 완전 훈남이야! (Jeo saram wanjeon hunnam-iya!) — "That guy is a total heartthrob!"

### 40. 깜놀 (kkamnol) — Startled / Shocked (casual)
- **From:** 깜짝 놀라다 (kkamjjak nollada, to be startled)
- **Context:** A cute way to say you were shocked or startled.
- **Example:** 깜놀했잖아! (Kkamnol-haetjana!) — "You scared me!"

## BONUS: Formal vs. Informal Speech Levels (Quick Guide)

### Understanding 존댓말 (jondaenmal) vs. 반말 (banmal)

Korean has multiple speech levels that signal respect, age hierarchy, and relationship closeness. This is CRUCIAL for understanding K-drama dialogue.

**존댓말 (Jondaenmal) — Polite/Formal Speech:**
- Used with: strangers, elders, bosses, anyone older, first meetings
- Verb endings: -요 (yo), -습니다 (seumnida)
- Example: 감사합니다 (gamsahamnida) — "Thank you" (very formal)
- Example: 고마워요 (gomawoyo) — "Thank you" (polite casual)

**반말 (Banmal) — Informal/Casual Speech:**
- Used with: close friends, people younger than you, people the same age (ONLY after mutual agreement)
- Verb endings: -아/어 (a/eo), no polite endings
- Example: 고마워 (gomawo) — "Thanks" (casual)

**Why This Matters in Boyfriend on Demand:**
- Mi-rae would use 존댓말 with her boss and coworkers initially
- The moment she switches to 반말 with Kyeong-nam signals growing intimacy — this is a HUGE relationship milestone in Korean culture
- With virtual boyfriends, the speech level choice reveals how "real" the relationships feel to her
- In Korean culture, asking "말 놓을까요?" (mal noeulkkayo? — "Shall we speak casually?") is a significant relationship step

**K-Drama Speech Level Moments to Watch For:**
- When characters switch from 존댓말 to 반말 = relationship leveling up
- When someone uses 반말 without permission = rude or aggressive
- When couples use both levels = complex emotional dynamics

### Quick Reference: Same Phrase in Different Levels

| Meaning | Formal (존댓말) | Casual (반말) |
|---------|----------------|--------------|
| Hello | 안녕하세요 (annyeonghaseyo) | 안녕 (annyeong) |
| Thank you | 감사합니다 (gamsahamnida) | 고마워 (gomawo) |
| I'm sorry | 죄송합니다 (joesonghamnida) | 미안해 (mianhae) |
| I love you | 사랑합니다 (saranghamnida) | 사랑해 (saranghae) |
| It's okay | 괜찮습니다 (gwaenchansseumnida) | 괜찮아 (gwaenchana) |
| Don't go | 가지 마세요 (gaji maseyo) | 가지 마 (gaji ma) |
| I miss you | 보고 싶습니다 (bogo sipseumnida) | 보고 싶어 (bogo sipeo) |

## LEARNING TIPS: Best Apps & Resources for Learning Korean (2026)

### Top Apps

1. **Talk To Me In Korean (TTMIK)** — Best for grammar foundations. Free lessons on YouTube + paid courses. The gold standard for structured Korean learning.

2. **LingoDeer** — Best for structured beginner lessons. Built specifically for Asian languages with clear progression.

3. **Duolingo Korean** — Good for daily habit building, gamified approach. Best as a supplement, not a primary resource.

4. **Anki** — Best for vocabulary retention. Uses spaced repetition flashcards. Free and highly customizable.

5. **HelloTalk** — Best for conversation practice. Chat with native Korean speakers with real-time corrections.

6. **Clozemaster** — Best for building vocabulary through context. Learn words in sentences, not isolation.

### Pro Tips for K-Drama Korean Learning

1. **Learn Hangul first** — Takes only 2-3 hours. It's one of the most logical writing systems ever created. Stop relying on romanization as soon as possible.

2. **Watch with Korean subtitles** — Not English! Korean subs help you connect spoken words to written Hangul.

3. **Use the "3-app stack"** — Combine: (a) structured grammar app, (b) flashcard system, (c) conversation practice.

4. **Shadow K-drama dialogue** — Pause and repeat what characters say. Copy their intonation and rhythm.

5. **Start a drama vocabulary notebook** — Write down new words you hear while watching. Review them the next day.

6. **Follow Korean social media** — Instagram, TikTok, and YouTube in Korean expose you to natural, current language.

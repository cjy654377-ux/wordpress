How It Started: The February 28 Operation

On the evening of February 28, 2026, the United States and Israel simultaneously launched air strikes targeting Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) facilities, air defense systems, drone launch sites, airfields, missile sites, naval installations, and nuclear facilities. The operation, which President Donald Trump described as proceeding "ahead of schedule," represents the largest coordinated Western military action against Iran in history.

The Israel Defense Forces (IDF) confirmed that approximately 600 "terrorist infrastructure sites" were dismantled using 2,500 munitions in the first wave alone. Israeli state media later confirmed that Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, age 86, was killed in air strikes targeting his office complex in Tehran, along with four dozen senior Iranian officials and military commanders.

Iran declared 40 days of national mourning and a seven-day national holiday following the confirmation of Khamenei's death on March 1, as state media broadcast footage of millions gathering in the streets.

Hezbollah Opens Second Front

Adding another dangerous dimension to the conflict, Iran-backed Hezbollah in Lebanon fired volleys of missiles at Israel on March 1, fulfilling longstanding threats to open a second front in any direct conflict between Iran and Israel. Israel responded with its own strikes on Lebanese targets, with Lebanese authorities reporting dozens of casualties.

This multi-front escalation has raised fears of a broader regional war that could draw in additional actors, including Iranian-backed militias in Iraq, Syria, and Yemen's Houthi forces who have already been disrupting Red Sea shipping for months.

US Casualties and Domestic Reaction

President Trump vowed to "avenge" the deaths of US service members, confirming that combat operations would continue. The president stated the operation could last "four weeks or less," while Pentagon officials provided daily briefings describing the systematic dismantling of Iran's military capabilities.

The loss of four American troops — the first US combat deaths in a major military operation since the Afghanistan withdrawal — has ignited fierce debate in Washington. Congressional leaders from both parties have demanded emergency briefings, while anti-war protests have erupted in major American cities.

Global Response

The UN Security Council convened an emergency session, though any resolution is expected to face vetoes. European allies have expressed mixed reactions, with the UK offering cautious support while France and Germany called for immediate ceasefire negotiations. Russia condemned the strikes as "an act of aggression," while China urged "maximum restraint" and offered to mediate.

What Happens Next?

As the operation enters its third day, several critical questions remain unanswered:

  • Duration: Trump says 4 weeks; military analysts suggest it could extend much longer if ground operations are considered.
  • Iranian leadership vacuum: With Khamenei dead and dozens of top officials killed, Iran's chain of command is in disarray. Who emerges as the new supreme leader will determine the trajectory of Iran's response.
  • Strait of Hormuz: Attacks on vessels transiting the strait have already begun, threatening 20% of global oil supply.
  • Nuclear question: Iran's nuclear facilities were among the targets. Whether the strikes successfully set back Iran's nuclear program — or accelerate a desperate push for a weapon — remains the most consequential unknown.
  • Regional expansion: Hezbollah, Houthis, and Iraqi militias could further widen the conflict.

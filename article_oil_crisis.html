The Oil Price Explosion: What Happened

Within hours of the first strikes on Iran, global oil markets erupted. US benchmark crude (WTI) surged approximately 8% to $72.41 per barrel, while the international benchmark Brent crude rocketed 8.48% to $79.05. This represents the largest single-day oil price jump since 2022, when Russia's invasion of Ukraine sent energy markets into turmoil.

The scale of the price move reflects a simple, terrifying calculation: Iran is the world's sixth-largest oil producer, producing roughly 3.2 million barrels per day. Any sustained military conflict involving Iran directly threatens not just Iranian output, but the entire flow of energy through the Persian Gulf.

The Strait of Hormuz: The World's Energy Chokepoint

Energy analysts have long called the Strait of Hormuz "the aorta of the global energy system" — and now that aorta is under direct threat. Roughly one-fifth of all global oil and a significant share of liquefied natural gas (LNG) flows through this narrow 21-mile-wide waterway between Iran and Oman.

In the hours following the strikes, attacks were reported on at least two vessels transiting the Strait of Hormuz, immediately restricting countries' ability to export oil. If Iran follows through on longstanding threats to fully close the strait — even temporarily — the consequences would be catastrophic for global energy supply.

"A full closure of Hormuz, even for days, would create a supply shock not seen since the 1973 oil embargo. We're talking about removing 17-18 million barrels per day from the market. No strategic reserve can compensate for that." — Kpler energy analytics

Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Kuwait, Qatar, Iraq, and Bahrain all rely on the Strait of Hormuz to export their oil and gas. Iran's retaliatory strikes on these very nations add a dangerous new dimension: even if the strait remains open, Gulf state export infrastructure itself is under attack.

Global Inflation: The Bigger Picture

The most far-reaching consequence may be the return of inflation at precisely the moment central banks were celebrating its decline. Oil price shocks have a well-documented ripple effect through the entire economy:

  • Transportation costs rise — everything shipped by truck, train, or plane gets more expensive
  • Manufacturing input costs spike — petrochemicals, plastics, and fertilizers all depend on oil and gas
  • Food prices increase — fertilizer costs directly impact agricultural output, threatening food security in developing nations
  • Central banks face impossible choices — cut rates to support growth, or keep them high to fight new inflation?
  • Consumer spending contracts — higher gas and food bills leave less disposable income for everything else
"A sustained $10/barrel increase in oil prices typically adds 0.3-0.4 percentage points to headline inflation across developed economies. If we see $90+ oil, the Fed's rate-cutting plans are effectively dead." — Bloomberg Economics

Shipping and Supply Chain Disruptions

Beyond oil, the conflict is already disrupting global shipping lanes. War risk insurance premiums for vessels transiting the Persian Gulf have skyrocketed overnight, adding significant costs to every container and tanker moving through the region. Combined with ongoing Houthi attacks in the Red Sea, two of the world's three most critical maritime chokepoints are now active conflict zones.

Shipping companies are already rerouting vessels around the Cape of Good Hope, adding 10-15 days to transit times between Asia and Europe. This means delayed deliveries, higher logistics costs, and potential shortages of everything from electronics to pharmaceuticals.

  • Review your budget for higher energy and food costs over the next 1-3 months

For Investors

  • Energy sector exposure may provide a hedge against broader market declines
  • Defense stocks historically outperform during sustained military conflicts
  • Avoid panic selling — military conflicts cause sharp but often temporary market drops
